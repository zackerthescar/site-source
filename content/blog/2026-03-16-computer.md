## The beginnings of my career

In 2017, freshly ending a chapter of my life, I bought a Raspberry Pi 3B.
In 2018, not long after earning my driver's license, I brought home the guts
of a computer. It had nothing but a CPU, motherboard, RAM, and a heatsink.
In hindsight, without those computers, I would probably have never gone thru 
CS50 on my own, eventually into school for Computer Science, and landed a 
job in the heart of the world. 

I had learned about port forwarding in 2012 when I was 11. I had been looking
at source code for PCem and GZDoom since about 2014, and I had been hosting
dedicated CS:GO servers in 2016. I would also be lying if I told you I was
some sort of child prodigy or had any sort of inate ability working the computer

(Sidenote: I abhor the word talent)</abbr>.

Yet I persisted. 

I hooked up the Raspberry Pi to just about every display I can find, and the
membrane Acer keyboard that partnered me since 2011. I figured out what an API
key is by hooking up Kodi to watch YouTube on disused CRTs. 

I bought sketchy hard drives from literal back alleys just so I can learn how 
to recover from RAID crashes with REAL DATA (please don't do this lol). 
I wrote Python to interface with Discord with some friends, and then promptly
freaked out about what a `screen` or `tmux` or `systemd` is.

I wrote Dockerfiles not knowing what a Linux environment even is. I wrote
systemd configs that I would never remember the names to. I learned to 
despise yaml purely from Docker Compose and not from a linguistic perspective.

I copied nginx configs not completely understanding what it did. I configured
Let's Encrypt and looked on with _infinite_ pride seeing that lock. Every 90
days or so I'd have to look up how to do it again. I never remembered the
correct `certbot` command. I never automated this until I moved to Caddy just
this year.

I followed the free CS50 guide. I wrote my first webapp served off of that
box in my own home.

My only regret back then was not being exposed to it sooner. I had 
spent most of my childhood and teenagehood neuroplasty on academics - things
I later found useful, but was first and foremost for getting a piece of
paper that impressed the parents. Hell, I was a debate kid. That was the
main thing my peers knew me for - farming debate creds. While I was _a_ 
computer whiz, I wasn't _the_ computer whiz. (Sidenote: I do have a theory
about why I was so good at debate, but you'll have to contact me somehow
to get _that_ story. It's not something I want to share to the world).

## The depressing part: this is a rare story

Anyways - even by 2018, _this_ story, in hindsight, was getting increasingly
rare. I remember having trouble even sourcing a computer to use as a sandbox.
The most popular fiber ISP in Malaysia deployed CG-NAT and I was not included
in that. I don't think my ISP ever forwarded me a DMCA alert for stepping on
basically every honeypot possible. I was surprised four years later hosting
my first Unix class that so few had even seen a terminal. In hindsight, I was
a fool. I was the one speaking in tongues.  

2018 was also the peak of the "serverless" craze - AWS Lambda had matured.
Firebase had just been made the face of Google's latest offering, Netlify and
Vercel were starting to take hold. Knowing what ports were was the domain of
IT and computer networking people, not makers and programmers. The only thing
that stopped me at the time was the prospect of putting in my billing details.

Combine all that with the ruthless SEO wars of the era - all the tutorials 
for building apps that were accessible to someone like me were 
_for serverless platforms_, and you'd have to dig to even find a tutorial
for something like Flask. Nobody in their right minds will tell you to host
Postgres yourself ("You're crazy, just use DynamoDB or Firestore"). 

I remember hearing people's struggles in the Intro to Software Engineering
class about writing the required Dockerfile. I one-shotted it even after
eschewing the Dockerfile for virtual machines (this was before bootc and
various other boot-from-container solutions). I looked down on everyone
else for not getting that done. I'm pretty sure the only things I did for
the final group project was the required explainer video and the Dockerfile.

And before you ask, yes, I remember reading about Aaron Swartz when I was 12
when the suicide happened.

The time just past the Aaron Swartz's ~~murder~~ suicide, once the dust had
settled, probably saw the biggest increase in "trust in the official
channels." Verify the news came from a reputable website. Check your
browser for the lock. Listen to official channels. These things were
drilled as canon to me in computer classes, and why not? They are good
_stepping stones_, but it seems like many have gotten a different message -
don't mess with the computer, it's for a privileged class with privileged
responsibilities.

After all, 4chan is (largely) self-hosted. Misinformation is DIY after all,
and only the official sources are to be trusted. I think Trump I did erode
public trust in _government_ sources, but definitely not the big, traditional
outlets. After all, the liberals had their CNNs and NBCs and the conservatives
had their Fox - no need to stray outside that norm!

I'm bombarding you with this because that's definitely how "the computer" was
taught to the classes I was in. Maybe not overtly, but that was definitely
the message that many got. Including me.

Perhaps that's why people sought an "out" to the beautiful mess of the computer - 
they don't want to be associated with "that dirtiness," and serverless provided
a service that, for a lack of a better analogy, offered an "official" avenue
to making applications online. 

Do NOT get me wrong - a lot of good came out of that era. Increased inclusivity
and visibility in FOSS, Codes of Conduct being _the norm_, the increased 
questioning of the term "meritocracy," were all good things the 2010s tech scene
brought us, not to mention all the revolutionary apps that were being honed in
this era. But we must examine this era like all others - for the good _and_ bad,
least we repeat the mistakes.
