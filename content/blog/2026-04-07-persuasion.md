This post will eventually tie back into a draft blog post that I'm writing,
follow this space. I also genuinely do not care if I end up losing friends on
this one - I cannot, as a principle, accept political violence, even when it
comes from my side of the aisle.

---

Over the weekend, two things happened in the Midwest that caught my attention.
My friends at [Fyra Stack](https://fyrastack.com/) went public with some really
cool offerings (like RISC-V compute _in North America_!) in Illinois, and a
lawmaker's house was shot up in Indiana. The only motive seemed to be on the
note the shooter left on the ground - "No Data Centers."

It's sad to see the world turn against compute as a resource for all, but I'm
gonna be honest in saying it's a long time coming. We as tech-conscious people,
who live and breathe this stuff, and (among the) first to pick up modern AI 
as a tool, have completely and utterly failed at communicating the pros and 
cons of our tools and the infrastructure we're trying to invest in, and what 
should have been constructive discussion has turned into straight partisan 
politics, to the point of my YIMBY friends who argue for dense housing and 
mass transportation _every day_ suddenly turning on data centers, simply 
because we have turned to the nationalist right for political support.

It didn't have to be like this. <!-- Killer whales, killer whales... -->

Truth be told, I wasn't even aware of the whole discourse around the "permanent
underclass" until I had stepped foot in San Francisco, and in hindsight I was
a fool - of course people are going to react that way (not to _justify_ political
violence, just merely trying to understand its origins). You've backed them
into a corner with your fantasies of recreating feudalism, and now a councilman's
door has bullet holes in it and the friendly neighborhood train guy is sucking up
to the people who make the train tracks hard to build.

When in reality the technology we're creating does democratize access to 
information and synthesizing it in ways that benefits _practically all jobs_, 
we've sold it as "we're going to take your jobs and turning you into serfs" and
some weird form of social casteization and Darwininsm. I've seen AI make the 
restaurant dinner cycle so much more efficient and the jobs of Target 
employees materially helped by these tools and yet the best we can do is "sorry
you're in the bottom rung and there's nothing to share with you."

We _have_ to engage in the deomcratic process more constructively and not score
quick wins, least we end up with deaths. We all make our own bed and lie in it,
and I fear the day I mourn the end of compute. 

---

The internet didn't _only_ enable big tech and the massive creepening into our
lives - it also allowed for lots of independent spaces to exist and flourish,
and many have built their refuge on the net as well.

AI will do much the same. It will not fix all our problems. It will exacerbate
them just like the internet and the smartphone did. We'll have to take the
good parts that help people and actively create a world one where we can
effectively deal with the social externalities at hand.
